## 1.1 What is Applied Statistics?

**Theoretical Explanation:**  
Applied statistics involves the **application of statistical theorems, formulas, rules, and laws to solve real-world problems**. In contrast, theoretical or mathematical statistics focuses on the development, derivation, and proof of these statistical concepts. The goal of applied statistics is to progress from identifying a problem, through data collection, to gaining information and knowledge, ultimately enabling informed decision-making.

**Real-world Application:**  
A company trying to understand customer satisfaction can:
- Survey a sample of customers (data collection)
- Analyze responses to identify common issues (knowledge discovery)
- Improve products or services based on insights (decision-making)

---
## 1.2 Types of Statistics

**Theoretical Explanation:**  
Applied statistics is broadly divided into:
- **Descriptive Statistics**: Organizing, displaying, and summarizing data using tables, graphs, and numerical summaries.
- **Inferential Statistics**: Using sample data to make generalizations, decisions, or predictions about a population.

**Real-world Application:**
- **Descriptive**: A marketing team creates a chart showing the age distribution of current customers.
- **Inferential**: Using sample age data to estimate the average age of all potential customers.

---
## 1.3 Population vs Sample

**Theoretical Explanation:**  
- **Population**: The complete set of all elements of interest.
- **Sample**: A subset drawn from the population.
- **Census**: A survey of every member of the population.
- **Sample Survey**: Collecting data from a sample.

**Mathematical Example:**
> Goal: Determine the per month family income of students at a university (JU)
- **Population**: All students of JU.
- **Sample**: A selected group of students from JU representing income characteristics.

## 1.4 Variables and Their Types

### What is a Variable?

A **variable** is a characteristic under study that varies from one element to another. It contrasts with a constant (fixed value). Variables can be:

- **Quantitative**
- **Qualitative**

---
### 1.4.1 Quantitative Variables

**Definition:** Variables that **can be measured numerically**.

#### Types of Quantitative Variables:

- **Discrete Variables**: Countable values (no intermediate values).
- **Continuous Variables**: Measurable values (infinite possible values within a range).

#### Examples:
- **Quantitative (General)**: Income, height, weight, gross sales.
- **Discrete**: Number of family members, cars sold, accidents per day.
- **Continuous**: Time taken for exams, monthly income, height, weight.

**Real-world Application:**
- **Discrete**: Number of daily sales transactions.
- **Continuous**: Daily revenue (e.g., $150.75).

---
### 1.4.2 Qualitative or Categorical Variables

**Definition:** Variables that **cannot be measured numerically**, but are classified into categories.

#### Examples:
- Gender
- Marital Status
- Brand of Computer
- Car Make
- Public Opinions

---
## 1.5 Cross-Section vs Time-Series Data

**Theoretical Explanation:**
- **Cross-Section Data**: Data on different elements at the same time.
- **Time-Series Data**: Data on the same element over time.

**Real-world Application:**
- **Cross-Section**: Income data of 100 households today.
- **Time-Series**: Monthly sales data over five years.

---
## 1.6 Scale of Measurement

Four types of measurement scales affect the kind of analysis possible.

---
### 1.6.1 Nominal Scale

**Definition:** Labels or names without quantitative value. Categories are mutually exclusive.

#### Examples:
- Gender (Male, Female)
- Hair Color (Brown, Black, Gray)
- Marital Status (Single, Married, Divorced)

---
### 1.6.2 Ordinal Scale

**Definition:** Ordered categories where the **order matters**, but differences are **not measurable**.

#### Examples:
- Mood scale: 1 = Very Unhappy, ..., 5 = Very Happy
- Satisfaction scale: 1 = Very Unsatisfied, ..., 5 = Very Satisfied

---
### 1.6.3 Interval Scale

**Definition:** Ordered and measurable differences, but **no true zero**. Negative values have meaning.

#### Example:
- **Celsius Temperature**:  
  - 10°C difference is meaningful  
  - 0°C ≠ absence of temperature  
  - Cannot say 40°C is twice as hot as 20°C

---
### 1.6.4 Ratio Scale

**Definition:** Ordered, measurable differences **with a true zero**. Supports all arithmetic operations.
#### Examples:
- Height
- Weight
- Time Duration
- Revenue

> A person who is 6 feet tall is truly twice as tall as a 3-foot-tall person.  
> Zero height = absence of height.
