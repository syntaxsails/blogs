## 2.1 Raw Data

**Theoretical Explanation:**  
Raw data refers to data that are recorded in the sequence in which they are collected, before any processing or ranking occurs. It can be quantitative or qualitative.

**Examples:**

- **Quantitative Raw Data**: Ages (in years) of 50 university students.    
- **Qualitative Raw Data**: Types of smartphones owned by 50 persons.    

---
## 2.2 Organizing and Graphing Qualitative Data

### 2.2.1 Frequency Distribution for Qualitative Data

**Theoretical Explanation:**  
A frequency distribution lists all the categories of a variable and counts how many elements fall into each category.

**Mathematical Example:**

**Problem:** 30 employees reported their job stress levels:

> somewhat, none, somewhat, very, very, none, very, somewhat, somewhat, very, somewhat, somewhat, very, somewhat, none, very, none, somewhat, somewhat, very, somewhat, somewhat, very, none, somewhat, very, very, somewhat, none, somewhat

**Steps:**

1. List unique categories: Very, Somewhat, None    
2. Tally the occurrences    
3. Count frequencies    

|Stress on Job|Frequency (f)|
|---|---|
|Very|10|
|Somewhat|14|
|None|6|
|**Total**|**30**|

**Findings:** 33.3% found jobs very stressful, 46.7% somewhat stressful, and 20% not stressful.

### 2.2.2 Relative Frequency and Percentage Distribution

**Formulas:**

$$\text{Relative Frequency} = \frac{f}{\text{Total } f}$$
$$\text{Percentage} = \text{Relative Frequency} \times 100$$

|Stress on Job|Frequency|Relative Frequency|Percentage|
|---|---|---|---|
|Very|10|0.333|33.3%|
|Somewhat|14|0.467|46.7%|
|None|6|0.200|20.0%|
|**Total**|30|**1.000**|**100%**|

### 2.2.3 Graphical Presentation of Qualitative Data

#### Bar Graphs

Bar heights represent frequency or relative frequency for each category. Bars are separated.

#### Pie Charts

- Use: Best for visualizing percentages.    
- Formula:    
$$\text{Angle size} = \text{Relative Frequency} \times 360^\circ$$

|Stress on Job|Relative Frequency|Angle Size|
|---|---|---|
|Very|0.333|119.88°|
|Somewhat|0.467|168.12°|
|None|0.200|72.00°|
|**Total**|**1.000**|**360°**|

### 2.3.2 Grouped Frequency Distribution

#### Sturges' Rule:

$$k = 1 + 3.322 \log_{10}(n)$$

$For$ $n=40:$
$$
\log_{10}(40) = 1.602 \\
k = 1 + 3.322 \times 1.602 = 6.32 \Rightarrow \text{round to 7}
$$


#### Class Width:

$$\text{Range} = 99 - 36 = 63$$
$$\text{Width} = \frac{63}{6.32} \approx 10$$

#### Frequency Distribution Example:

|Class Interval|Frequency|
|---|---|
|30–39|3|
|40–49|1|
|50–59|8|
|60–69|10|
|70–79|7|
|80–89|7|
|90–99|4|
|**Total**|**40**|

### 2.3.3 Class Boundaries, Width, and Midpoint

|Class Limits|Boundaries|Width|Midpoint|
|---|---|---|---|
|30–39|29.5–39.5|10|34.5|


$$\text{Class Boundaries: } \frac{39 + 40}{2} = 39.5$$ $$\text{Width: } 39.5 - 29.5 = 10 $$ $$\text{Midpoint: } \frac{30 + 39}{2} = 34.5$$

### 2.3.4 Histograms

- Bars touch to indicate continuous data    
- Horizontal axis: Class boundaries    
- Vertical axis: Frequencies    

### 2.3.5 Polygons

- Points plotted at class midpoints    
- Lines connect points    
- Ends start and end at zero frequency    

### 2.3.6 Shapes of Histograms

- **Symmetric**: Mean = Median = Mode    
- **Skewed Right**: Mode < Median < Mean    
- **Skewed Left**: Mean < Median < Mode    
- **Uniform**: All frequencies equal    

**Real-world Application:** Income distributions often skew right.

### 2.3.9 Other Plots

#### Dot Plot

- Dots stacked above each category value    
- Good for small datasets    

**Application:** Baby vaccinations by colony.

#### Time Series Plot

- Line graph with time on x-axis    
- Tracks trends or patterns    

**Application:** Monthly temperature over 2 years.
