## 3.1 Measures of Central Tendency

**Theoretical Explanation:**
A **measure of central tendency** is a **single value that attempts to describe a set of data by identifying the central position within that set** . It acts as a summary statistic representing the center point or typical value of a dataset, indicating where most values in a distribution fall. The three main measures are the **mean, median, and mode**. Each provides a different indication of the typical or central value.

## 3.2 Arithmetic Mean (Mean)

**Theoretical Explanation:**
The **arithmetic mean** (or simply **mean**) is the most commonly used measure of central tendency. It is calculated by summing all values in the data set and dividing by the number of values. It includes all values of the data set in its calculation, meaning any change in a value will affect the mean.
*   **Population Mean ($\mu$):** For a population of $N$ observations, $\mu = \frac{\sum x_i}{N}$.
*   **Sample Mean ($\bar{x}$):** For a sample of $n$ observations, $\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n}$.


#### 3.2.1 Calculating Mean for Individual Observations

**Mathematical Example/Detailed Steps:**
**Problem:** The ages (in years) of all eight employees of a small company are: 53, 32, 61, 27, 39, 44, 49, 57. Find the mean age.

**Steps:**
1.  **Identify if it's population or sample:** Since it includes *all* eight employees, it's a population (`N = 8`) .
2.  **Sum all values:** $\sum x_i = 53+32+61+27+39+44+49+57 = 362$.
3.  **Divide by N:** $\mu = \frac{362}{8} = \textbf{45.25 years}$.

**Findings:** The mean age of employees is 45.25 years, or 45 years and 3 months.

**Real-world Application (Impact of Outliers):**
The mean is **heavily influenced by outliers**. An **outlier** is an observation that is unusually large or small compared to the rest of the data.
**Problem:** Philanthropic giving's (in million dollars) by six companies: CVS (22.4), Best Buy (31.8), Staples (19.8), Walgreen (9.0), Lowe's (27.5), Wal-Mart (337.9). Wal-Mart's contribution (337.9) is an outlier. Show its impact on the mean.

**Steps:**

Mean without outlier (trimmed mean):

$$ \bar{x}_{\text{trimmed}} = \frac{22.4+31.8+19.8+9.0+27.5}{5} = \frac{110.5}{5} = \textbf{22.1 million usd} $$

Mean with outlier: 

$$ \bar{x}_{\text{full}} = \frac{22.4+31.8+19.8+9.0+27.5+337.9}{6} = \frac{448.4}{6} = \textbf{74.73 million usd} $$

**Findings:** Including Wal-Mart's contribution caused **more than a threefold increase in the mean**, from $22.1 million to $74.73 million. This demonstrates that the mean is not always the best measure of central tendency when outliers are present. In such cases, a **trimmed mean** (which eliminates a percentage of extreme values before averaging) or the median might be more appropriate.

#### 3.2.2 Calculating Mean for Ungrouped Frequency Distribution

**Theoretical Explanation:**
For an ungrouped frequency distribution, the mean is calculated by **multiplying each observation value ($x_i$) by its frequency ($f_i$), summing these products, and then dividing by the total number of observations ($\sum f_i$ or $n$)**.
*   For population: $\mu = \frac{\sum (f_i \times x_i)}{N}$.
*   For sample: $\bar{x} = \frac{\sum (f_i \times x_i)}{n}$.

**Mathematical Example/Detailed Steps:**
**Problem:** A sample of 50 personal property insurance policies showed the following number of claims over two years. Calculate the mean number of claims.

| No. of claims ($x_i$) | No. of policies ($f_i$) |
| :-------------------- | :---------------------- |
| 0                     | 21                      |
| 1                     | 13                      |
| 2                     | 5                       |
| 3                     | 4                       |
| 4                     | 2                       |
| 5                     | 3                       |
| 6                     | 2                       |
| **Sum**               | **50** ($n$)            |

**Steps:**
1.  **Create a new column** for $f_i \times x_i$.
2.  **Calculate $f_i \times x_i$** for each row.
3.  **Sum the $f_i \times x_i$ column**.
4.  **Divide the sum by the total frequency** ($n=50$).

**Table & Calculation:**

| No. of claims ($x_i$) | No. of policies ($f_i$) | $f_i \times x_i$ |
|------------------------|--------------------------|------------------|
| 0                      | 21                       | 0                |
| 1                      | 13                       | 13               |
| 2                      | 5                        | 10               |
| 3                      | 4                        | 12               |
| 4                      | 2                        | 8                |
| 5                      | 3                        | 15               |
| 6                      | 2                        | 12               |
| **Sum**                | **50** ($n$)             | **70**           |

Mean number of claims = $\bar{x} = \frac{\sum (f_i \times x_i)}{n} = \frac{70}{50} = \textbf{1.4 claims}$.

**Findings:** The average number of claims per policy in this sample is 1.4.

#### 3.2.3 Calculating Mean for Grouped Frequency Distribution

**Theoretical Explanation:**
For grouped data, the mean is calculated by **multiplying the midpoint of each class ($m_i$) by its frequency ($f_i$), summing these products, and then dividing by the total frequency ($\sum f_i$ or $n$)**.
*   For population: $\mu = \frac{\sum (f_i \times m_i)}{N}$.
*   For sample: $\bar{x} = \frac{\sum (f_i \times m_i)}{n}$.

**Mathematical Example/Detailed Steps:**
**Problem:** Calculate the arithmetic mean of the "days to maturity 40 short-term investments" data.

| Class interval | No. of investments ($f_i$) |
| :------------- | :------------------------- |
| 30—39          | 3                          |
| 40—49          | 1                          |
| 50—59          | 8                          |
| 60—69          | 10                         |
| 70—79          | 7                          |
| 80—89          | 7                          |
| 90—99          | 4                          |
| **Total**      | **40** ($n$)               |

**Steps:**
1.  **Find the midpoint ($m_i$)** for each class: $m_i = \frac{\text{Lower limit + Upper limit}}{2}$.
2.  **Create a new column** for $f_i \times m_i$.
3.  **Calculate $f_i \times m_i$** for each row.
4.  **Sum the $f_i \times m_i$ column**.
5.  **Divide the sum by the total frequency** ($n=40$).

**Table & Calculation:**

| Class interval | Midpoint ($m_i$) | Frequency ($f_i$) | $f_i \times m_i$ |
| :------------- | :--------------- | :---------------- | :--------------- |
| 30—39          | 34.5             | 3                 | 103.5            |
| 40—49          | 44.5             | 1                 | 44.5             |
| 50—59          | 54.5             | 8                 | 436              |
| 60—69          | 64.5             | 10                | 645              |
| 70—79          | 74.5             | 7                 | 521.5            |
| 80—89          | 84.5             | 7                 | 591.5            |
| 90—99          | 94.5             | 4                 | 378              |
| **Total**      |                  | **40**            | **2720**         |

Mean = $\bar{x} = \frac{\sum (f_i \times m_i)}{n} = \frac{2720}{40} = \textbf{68.00 days}$.

**Findings:** The average maturity period for these short-term investments is 68 days.

#### 3.2.4 Weighted Mean

**Theoretical Explanation:**
A **weighted mean** is a special type of mean used when **different observations in a data set have different levels of importance or "weights"** ($w_i$).
**Formula:** $\bar{x} = \frac{\sum (w_i \times x_i)}{\sum w_i}$.

**Real-world Application (Grade Point Average - GPA):**
**Problem:** A student completed 15 credit hours and received grades with corresponding grade points and credit hours. Calculate the student's semester GPA.

| Course      | Grade | Grade Point ($x_i$) | Credit hours ($w_i$) |
| :---------- | :---- | :------------------ | :------------------- |
| English     | A     | 4                   | 3                    |
| Math        | B     | 3                   | 3                    |
| Biology lab | C     | 2                   | 4                    |
| Spanish     | D     | 1                   | 5                    |
| **Total**   |       |                     | **15**               |

**Steps:**
1.  **Multiply each grade point ($x_i$) by its corresponding credit hours ($w_i$)** to get $w_i \times x_i$.
2.  **Sum the $w_i \times x_i$ column**.
3.  **Sum the credit hours ($w_i$)**.
4.  **Divide the sum of ($w_i \times x_i$) by the sum of $w_i$**.

**Table & Calculation:**

| Course      | Grade Point ($x_i$) | Credit hours ($w_i$) | $w_i \times x_i$ |
| :---------- | :------------------ | :------------------- | :--------------- |
| English     | 4                   | 3                    | 12               |
| Math        | 3                   | 3                    | 9                |
| Biology lab | 2                   | 4                    | 8                |
| Spanish     | 1                   | 5                    | 5                |
| **Total**   |                     | **15**               | **34**           |

GPA = $\bar{x} = \frac{34}{15} = \textbf{2.266...} \approx \textbf{2.3}$.

**Findings:** The student's correct GPA is 2.3, not 2.5 (which would be obtained by a simple mean ignoring credit hours). This illustrates that weighted means are necessary when elements contribute unequally.

### 3.3 Median

**Theoretical Explanation:**
The **median** is the **middlemost observation in a set of ordered (increasing or decreasing) observations**. It divides a ranked data set into two equal parts. The median is preferred over the mean as a measure of central tendency for data sets that contain outliers because **it is not influenced by outliers**.

#### 3.3.1 Calculating Median for Individual Observations

**Theoretical Explanation:**
1.  **Rank the data set** in increasing order.
2.  **Find the middle term(s)**. The median is located at the $\frac{n+1}{2}^{th}$ ordered position.
    *   If **n is odd**, the median is the value of the single middle term.
    *   If **n is even**, the median is the average of the two middle terms.

**Mathematical Example/Detailed Steps:**
**Problem 1 (Odd n):** Prices (in thousands of dollars) of seven houses: 312, 257, 421, 289, 526, 374, 497. Find the median.

**Steps:**
1.  **Rank the data:** 257, 289, 312, 374, 421, 497, 526.
2.  **Find middle term position:** $n=7$, so position is $\frac{7+1}{2} = 4^{th}$ term.
3.  **Identify median:** The $4^{th}$ term in the ranked data is **374**.

**Findings:** The median house price is $374,000. Half the houses cost less than $374,000, and half cost more.

**Problem 2 (Even n):** 2020 profits (billions of dollars) of 12 companies: 8, 12, 7, 17, 14, 45, 10, 13, 17, 13, 9, 11. Find the median.

**Steps:**
1.  **Rank the data:** 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, **12, 13**, 13, 14, 17, 17, 45.
2.  **Find middle term position:** $n=12$, so position is $\frac{12+1}{2} = 6.5^{th}$ observation. This means between the $6^{th}$ and $7^{th}$ observations.
3.  **Identify middle terms:** The $6^{th}$ term is 12, and the $7^{th}$ term is 13.
4.  **Calculate median:** Median = $\frac{12+13}{2} = \textbf{12.5}$ billion dollars.

**Findings:** The median profit is $12.5 billion.

#### 3.3.2 Calculating Median from Ungrouped Frequency Distribution

**Mathematical Example/Detailed Steps:**
**Problem:** The number of family members of 30 families. Find the median.

| Number of family members | Number of families (f) |
| :----------------------- | :--------------------- |
| 2                        | 2                      |
| 3                        | 8                      |
| 4                        | 10                     |
| 5                        | 6                      |
| 6                        | 3                      |
| 7                        | 1                      |
| **Total**                | **30** ($n$)           |

**Steps:**
1.  **Determine median position:** $n=30$, so position is $\frac{30+1}{2} = 15.5^{th}$ observation. This means the median is between the $15^{th}$ and $16^{th}$ observations.
2.  **Create a cumulative frequency column** to locate the position.
3.  **Identify the value** corresponding to the median position.

**Table & Calculation:**

| Number of family members | Number of families (f) | Cumulative frequency |
| :----------------------- | :--------------------- | :------------------- |
| 2                        | 2                      | 2                    |
| 3                        | 8                      | 10 (1st-10th obs)    |
| 4                        | 10                     | 20 (11th-20th obs)   |
| 5                        | 6                      | 26                   |
| 6                        | 3                      | 29                   |
| 7                        | 1                      | 30                   |

**Findings:** Since both the $15^{th}$ and $16^{th}$ observations fall within the "4" family members category (cumulative frequency up to 20), the median number of family members is **4**.

#### 3.3.3 Calculating Median from Grouped Data

**Theoretical Explanation:**
For grouped data, the median is approximated using the formula:
$\text{Median} = L_l + \frac{(\frac{n}{2} - c)}{f} \times h$ 
Where:
*   $L_l$ = Lower limit of the median class.
*   $n$ = Total frequency.
*   $c$ = Cumulative frequency of the class preceding the median class.
*   $f$ = Frequency of the median class.
*   $h$ = Width of the median class.

**Mathematical Example/Detailed Steps:**
**Problem:** Calculate the median days of maturity for the 40 short-term investments (grouped data).

| Class interval | Frequency ($f_i$) | Cumulative frequency |
| :------------- | :---------------- | :------------------- |
| 30—39          | 3                 | 3                    |
| 40—49          | 1                 | 4                    |
| 50—59          | 8                 | 12                   |
| 60—69          | 10                | 22                   |
| 70—79          | 7                 | 29                   |
| 80—89          | 7                 | 36                   |
| 90—99          | 4                 | 40                   |
| **Total**      | **40** ($n$)      |                      |

**Steps:**
1.  **Determine median position:** $n=40$, so $\frac{n}{2} = \frac{40}{2} = 20^{th}$ observation. (Note: The source sometimes uses (n+1)/2, sometimes n/2 for grouped data. For formula use, n/2 is common).
2.  **Identify median class:** The $20^{th}$ observation falls into the `60-69` class (cumulative frequency reaches 22 in this class). So, the median class is **60-69**.
3.  **Identify parameters for formula:**
    *   $L_l$ (lower limit of median class) = **60**.
    *   $c$ (cumulative frequency of class preceding median class) = **12** (cumulative frequency of 50-59 class).
    *   $f$ (frequency of median class) = **10**.
    *   $h$ (width of median class) = `69.5 - 59.5 = 10`.
4.  **Apply the formula:**
    $\text{Median} = 60 + \frac{(20 - 12)}{10} \times 10$
    $\text{Median} = 60 + \frac{8}{10} \times 10$
    $\text{Median} = 60 + 8 = \textbf{68 days}$.

**Findings:** The median number of days for short-term investments to mature is 68 days.

### 3.4 Mode

**Theoretical Explanation:**  
The **mode**, if it exists, is the **most frequently occurring value** in a data set. A data set can have no mode, one mode (unimodal), two modes (bimodal), or more than two modes (multimodal). The mode can be used for both quantitative and qualitative data.

#### 3.4.1 Calculating Mode for Individual Observations

**Mathematical Example/Detailed Steps:**  
**Problem 1 (Unimodal):** Speeds (mph) of eight cars: 77, 82, 74, 81, 79, 84, 74, 78. Find the mode.

**Steps:**
1. **Count frequencies:** 74 appears twice; other values appear once.    
2. **Identify most frequent value:** 74 is the most frequent.    

**Findings:** Mode = **74 miles per hour**. This is a unimodal distribution.

**Problem 2 (Bimodal):** Speeds (mph) of eight cars: 77, 82, 74, 81, 79, 84, 74, 78, 82. Find the mode.

**Steps:**
1. **Count frequencies:** 74 appears twice, 82 appears twice; other values appear once.    
2. **Identify most frequent values:** 74 and 82 both appear with the highest frequency.    

**Findings:** Modes = **74 and 82 miles per hour**. This is a bimodal distribution.

#### 3.4.2 Calculating Mode from Ungrouped Frequency Distribution

**Mathematical Example/Detailed Steps:**  
**Problem:** The number of family members of 30 families. Find the mode.

|Number of family members|Number of families (f)|
|:--|:--|
|2|2|
|3|8|
|4|10|
|5|6|
|6|3|
|7|1|

**Steps:**
1. **Scan the frequency column** to find the highest frequency.    
2. **Identify the corresponding value**.    

**Findings:** The highest frequency is 10, which corresponds to "4" family members. Therefore, the mode is **4** family members, and it's a unimodal distribution.

#### 3.4.3 Calculating Mode from Grouped Frequency Distribution

**Theoretical Explanation:**  
For grouped data, the mode is found in the class with the highest frequency (the **modal class**). To determine a single value for the mode, the following formula is used:  
$$ \text{Mode} = L_{M0} + \frac{d_1}{d_1 + d_2} \times w$$
Where:

- $L_{M0}$ = Lower limit of the modal class    
- $d1$ = Frequency of the modal class minus the frequency of the class _preceding_ the modal class   
- $d2$ = Frequency of the modal class minus the frequency of the class _following_ the modal class    
- $w$ = Width of the modal class interval    

**Mathematical Example/Detailed Steps:**  
**Problem:** Calculate the mode days of maturity for the 40 short-term investments (grouped data).

| Class interval | Frequency ($f_i$) |
| :------------- | :---------------- |
| 30—39          | 3                 |
| 40—49          | 1                 |
| 50—59          | 8                 |
| 60—69          | 10                |
| 70—79          | 7                 |
| 80—89          | 7                 |
| 90—99          | 4                 |

**Steps:**

1. **Identify modal class:** The class with the highest frequency is `60-69` ($f_i$=10).    
2. **Identify parameters for formula:**    
    - $L_{M0}$ (lower limit of modal class) = **60**        
    - $d_1$ = Frequency of modal class (10) - Frequency of preceding class (8) $=10-8 = \textbf{2}$        
    - $d_2$ = Frequency of modal class (10) - Frequency of following class (7) $= 10-7 = \textbf{3}$        
    - $w$ (width of modal class) = **10**        
3. **Apply the formula:**  
    $\text{Mode} = 60 + \frac{2}{2 + 3} \times 10$  
    $\text{Mode} = 60 + \frac{2}{5} \times 10$  
    $\text{Mode} = 60 + 0.4 \times 10$
    $\text{Mode} = 60 + 4 = \textbf{64 days}$    

**Findings:** The mode number of days to mature the short-term investment is 64 days.

### 3.5 Overall Observations & Relationships Among Measures

**Theoretical Explanation:**  
No single measure of central tendency is universally "best"; their suitability depends on the situation and data characteristics.

- The **mean** is often the most used measure for data without outliers because it incorporates every value.    
- The **median** is superior when a data set contains outliers because it is not affected by extreme values.    
- The **mode** is simple to locate but less useful in practical applications.    
- The mean and median are used only for quantitative data, while the mode can be used for both quantitative and qualitative data.    

Knowing the mean, median, and mode can also provide insight into the **shape of a frequency distribution curve**.

1. **Symmetric Distribution (one peak):** The **mean, median, and mode are identical** and lie at the center of the distribution.    
2. **Skewed-to-the-Right Distribution (positive skew):** The **mean is the largest**, the **mode is the smallest**, and the **median lies between them**. This is because outliers in the right tail pull the mean to the right. The relationship is: **Mode < Median < Mean**.    
3. **Skewed-to-the-Left Distribution (negative skew):** The **mean is the smallest**, the **mode is the largest**, and the **median lies between them**. Outliers in the left tail pull the mean to the left. The relationship is: **Mean < Median < Mode**.    

### 3.6 Geometric Mean (GM)

**Theoretical Explanation:**  
The **Geometric Mean (GM)** is an average value that represents the central tendency by taking the **nth root of the product of n values**. It differs from the arithmetic mean, which adds values. GM is particularly useful for calculating average growth rates over multiple periods.

- For raw data: $GM = \sqrt[n]{x_1 \times x_2 \times \dots \times x_n}$
    
- For frequency distribution: $GM = (\prod_{i=1}^{k} x_i^{f_i})^{1/n}, where n=∑fin = \sum f_i$    
    - Can be calculated using logarithms: $GM = \text{Antilog} \left( \frac{\sum f_i \log x_i}{n} \right)$        
- **Cannot be used when data contains 0 or negative values**    

**Real-world Application/Mathematical Example:**  
**Problem:** Calculate the GM for the following grouped frequency distribution of weights.

|Weights of ear heads (g)|No of ear heads (f)|Mid (xix_i)|Log xix_i|filog⁡xif_i \log x_i|
|:--|:--|:--|:--|:--|
|60-80|22|70|1.845|40.59|
|80-100|38|90|1.954|74.25|
|100-120|45|110|2.041|91.85|
|120-140|35|130|2.114|73.99|
|140-160|20|150|2.176|43.52|
|**Total**|**160** (nn)|||**324.2**|

**Steps:**

1. **Find midpoints ($x_i$)** for each class.    
2. **Calculate $\log x_i$** for each midpoint.    
3. **Calculate $\log x_i$** for each row.    
4. **Sum the $\log x_i$ column** and get $n \text{(total frequency)}$.    
5. **Apply the formula:** $GM = \text{Antilog} \left( \frac{\sum f_i \log x_i}{n} \right)$  
    $\text{GM} = \text{Antilog} \left( \frac{324.2}{160} \right)$  
    $\text{GM} = \text{Antilog} (2.02625)$
    $\text{GM} = \textbf{106.23}$    

**Findings:** The geometric mean weight of ear heads is 106.23g.

**Advantages:** Based on all observations, rigidly defined, less affected by fluctuations.  
**Disadvantages:** Not easily understood by non-mathematicians, becomes zero if any observation is zero, imaginary if any observation is negative.

### 3.7 Harmonic Mean (HM)

**Theoretical Explanation:**  
The **Harmonic Mean (HM)** is defined as the **reciprocal of the arithmetic mean of the reciprocals of the observations**. It is typically used to calculate average values when the values are expressed as a rate or ratio (value/unit), such as speed (km/hour).

- For raw data: $HM = \frac{n}{\sum (\frac{1}{x_i})}$    
- For frequency distribution (grouped or ungrouped, $x_i$ is midpoint for grouped): $HM = \frac{\sum f_i}{\sum (\frac{f_i}{x_i})}$    

**Real-world Application/Mathematical Example:**  
**Problem:** A man travels from Jaipur to Agra. He travels at 50 km/hr (1st hour), 64 km/hr (2nd hour), 80 km/hr (3rd hour), and 55 km/hr (4th hour). Find the average speed.

**Steps:**
1. Identify the values ($x_i$) and number of observations ($n=4$).    
2. Calculate the reciprocal of each speed ($1/x_i$).    
3. Sum the reciprocals.    
4. Apply the $HM$ formula.    

**Calculation:**  
$x_1=50, x_2=64, x_3=80, x_4=55$  
$\frac{1}{x_1} = \frac{1}{50} = 0.02$  
$\frac{1}{x_2} = \frac{1}{64} = 0.015625$  
$\frac{1}{x_3} = \frac{1}{80} = 0.0125$  
$\frac{1}{x_4} = \frac{1}{55} = 0.0181818$  
$\sum (\frac{1}{x_i}) = 0.0663068$

$HM = \frac{4}{0.0663068} \approx \textbf{60.32 km/hr}$

**Findings:** The average speed is approximately 60.32 km/hr. HM is appropriate because speed is a rate.

**Limitations:** Difficult to calculate and understand, requires all values, usually results in a value not present in the series.

### 3.8 Relationship Among A.M., G.M., and H.M.

**Theoretical Explanation:**  
For any distribution where the original items are different, the Arithmetic Mean (A.M.), Geometric Mean (G.M.), and Harmonic Mean (H.M.) will differ and will always be in the following order:  
**$A.M.\ge G.M.\ge H.M.$**
