# Time and Effort Estimation in Project Management

As a project manager, your responsibility is to identify, assign, and estimate the completion time for tasks to determine the overall project schedule.

## Time Estimation vs. Effort Estimation

- **Time Estimation**: A prediction of the total duration required to complete a task, including both active and inactive time.    
- **Effort Estimation**: A prediction of the amount and difficulty of active work required for a person to complete a task.    

**Example**: Painting a wall might have an effort estimation of 30 minutes (active painting) but a time estimation of 24 hours (including 23.5 hours of inactive drying time).

Understanding the difference is crucial for efficient resource allocation, as idle time within a task allows team members to work on other assignments.

## Avoiding Unrealistic Effort Estimates

Underestimating effort, often due to excessive optimism, can negatively impact a project schedule by overlooking potential risks and setbacks. To avoid this, communicate with the teammates assigned to each task. They possess the most realistic understanding of the work required and can provide accurate estimates.

**Example (Plant Pals Project)**:

- Initial assumption: Creating a contact list of top customers takes one day.    
- Considering sub-tasks: Meeting with the global sales team, gathering contact information, determining language preferences, and building a spreadsheet.    
- Teammate's estimate: Two days, double the original expectation, due to a deeper understanding of the nuances involved.    

While you can ask follow-up questions or gently push back on estimates, the task owner's input is invaluable for accuracy.

## Buffers: Mitigating Inaccurate Estimates

Effort estimates are not always accurate, and unexpected delays can occur (e.g., a sales team being out of office).

**Buffers** are extra time added to a task or project to account for such slowdowns or delays. Buffers provide leeway, preventing the project from falling off track when delays arise.

There are two types of buffers:

1. **Task Buffers**: Extra time added to a specific task.    
    - **Primary Use**: For tasks outside the project team's control (e.g., waiting for a vendor's estimate).        
    - **Limited Use**: Should be used sparingly for tasks within the team's control, only for those that are difficult or unpredictable (e.g., plant growth time). Adding buffers to every task can unnecessarily lengthen the schedule.        
2. **Project Buffers**: Extra time added to the overall project schedule, typically at the end.    
    - This buffer can be used as needed throughout the project to account for missed deadlines by teammates.        
    - **Google Example**: A project manager used buffers to account for a new hire's need for more testing time, ensuring a realistic project timeline and preventing project failure due to unrealistic expectations.        

Time estimation, effort estimation, and the strategic use of buffers are crucial for building realistic project plans and achieving project goals.
