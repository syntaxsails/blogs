It is human nature to underestimate the time and effort required for tasks, a phenomenon known as the planning fallacy. This tendency stems from

**optimism bias**, where individuals believe they are less likely to experience negative events. While optimism is a positive personal trait, it can be a deficiency for project managers during the planning phase.

## The Planning Fallacy and Optimism Bias

First introduced by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky in 1977, the

**planning fallacy** describes our tendency to underestimate the time, costs, and risks associated with completing a task due to optimism bias. Optimism bias leads a person to believe they are less likely to experience a negative event. For example, when planning to walk a dog between meetings, one might optimistically believe they can do it faster than possible, overlooking factors like weather, other dogs, or distractions. This could lead to being late for the next meeting.

The planning fallacy can affect anyone, regardless of their experience with similar tasks. Project managers, whether new or experienced, must be careful not to underestimate the time needed for each task. The goal is to be "optimistically realistic": pushing for the best outcomes while planning for the proper time to accomplish each task.

## Avoiding the Planning Fallacy: A Case Study

Failing to account for the planning fallacy can significantly impact project execution, potentially leading to insufficient time for task completion, rework, and missed opportunities.

Consider David, a project manager for a home construction project. His Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) includes milestones for Foundation, Construction, and Finishing, with associated tasks such as Blueprint, Labor to dig, Order materials, Labor to build structure, Labor to build roof, Plumbing, Electrical, Paint, and Landscaping.

David's initial time estimates were:

- **Foundation**: 2 weeks    
- **Construction**: 4 weeks    
- **Adjustments**: 4 weeks    

This totaled 10 weeks, meeting his delivery requirement. An unaware project manager might consider this plan solid.

However, David is mindful of the planning fallacy. He carefully examines his estimates, considering risks like weather delays or sick crew members. He consults his team members and other stakeholders to uncover additional potential risks to the project timeline. After gathering information, he adjusts his time estimates by adding task buffers to account for these risks.

## Key Takeaways

Being on the lookout for "what-ifs" is a crucial project management skill. Considering situations that could affect on-time project completion helps overcome the planning fallacy. Utilize your project team as a resource to uncover possible risks. Always strive to be "optimistically realistic"—push for the best outcome while realistically planning the time required for each task.
